\begin{abstract}
We present a complete pipeline for model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) using frozen video foundation models as the sole perceptual backbone. Building on V-JEPA~2 (ViT-L, 325M parameters), which is pretrained on internet video and \emph{never fine-tuned}, we learn a dynamics MLP and a task-aligned reward head in the frozen latent space, then deploy a Dyna-style online learning loop with Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) planning. Through a systematic series of 10 experiments on the DeepMind Control Suite walker-walk task, we progressively identify and resolve three key bottlenecks: (1)~reward misalignment in proxy-based planning, solved by a learned reward head that pushes win rate from 50\% to 80\%; (2)~reward head overfitting during online fine-tuning, characterized through ablation studies; and (3)~dynamics model catastrophic forgetting, solved by a mixed replay buffer combining offline and on-policy data. Our final system achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} against a random baseline on walker-walk, with all learning confined to lightweight MLPs ($<$1M parameters) operating on frozen V-JEPA features. The entire experimental campaign costs under \$30 in cloud GPU compute. Our results demonstrate that video foundation models pretrained for visual understanding contain representations rich enough for continuous control, without any task-specific encoder training.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The dominant paradigm in visual robot learning trains task-specific encoders from scratch, requiring millions of interactions per task~\citep{hafner2020dreamer, hansen2022td-mpc}. A compelling alternative is to leverage \emph{foundation models}---large networks pretrained on diverse internet-scale data---as frozen perceptual backbones. If such models encode sufficient spatial and dynamical information, one could bypass encoder training entirely and focus learning on lightweight control modules.

Video foundation models are particularly promising because they are trained to predict visual dynamics---the very quantity needed for model-based control. V-JEPA~2~\citep{bardes2024vjepa} learns joint embeddings of video clips via self-supervised prediction in latent space, yielding representations that capture object position, motion, and scene dynamics without requiring labelled data or pixel-level reconstruction. LeCun's ``path towards autonomous machine intelligence''~\citep{lecun2022path} proposed exactly this architecture: a world model built on self-supervised latent prediction.

However, the original V-JEPA paper evaluates representations \emph{only} on classification and action recognition benchmarks---linear probes for downstream perception tasks. \textbf{No prior work has used V-JEPA representations for control, planning, or reinforcement learning.}

This paper asks: \textbf{Can a frozen V-JEPA~2 encoder, combined with learned dynamics and reward models plus online Dyna learning, control a simulated robot to walk?}

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate that frozen V-JEPA~2 features support accurate dynamics prediction in continuous control (MSE $<$0.024 in 384-dim latent space).
    \item We introduce a task-aligned reward head trained in V-JEPA latent space, breaking a 50\% performance ceiling observed with proxy reward signals.
    \item We propose a Dyna-style online learning loop with a mixed replay buffer that achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} on walker-walk, the first demonstration of competitive control using frozen video foundation model features.
    \item We provide a detailed empirical analysis of reward head overfitting and dynamics forgetting across 10 experiments, offering practical guidance for deploying frozen encoders in MBRL.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{World Models for Control.}
Dreamer~\citep{hafner2020dreamer} and DreamerV3~\citep{hafner2023dreamerv3} learn latent dynamics models and train actor-critic policies ``in imagination.'' TD-MPC~\citep{hansen2022td-mpc} combines temporal-difference learning with MPC and learned models. PETS~\citep{chua2018pets} introduced probabilistic ensemble dynamics for robust planning. All of these methods learn both the encoder and the dynamics model end-to-end. Our work uses a \emph{frozen} encoder, learning only dynamics and reward modules.

\paragraph{Foundation Models for Robotics.}
R3M~\citep{nair2022r3m} and VIP~\citep{ma2023vip} pretrain visual encoders on Ego4D video for robotic manipulation. RT-2~\citep{brohan2023rt2} uses vision-language models for end-to-end control. These approaches either fine-tune the encoder or use it for representation learning, not for direct world modelling. Our approach uses a \emph{video prediction} model (V-JEPA~2) as a frozen backbone for latent dynamics and reward prediction.

\paragraph{Dyna Architecture.}
Sutton's Dyna~\citep{sutton1991dyna} introduced the idea of interleaving real experience with model-generated experience for planning. Modern incarnations~\citep{hafner2020dreamer} use deep neural networks for both the model and the policy. Our Dyna loop is distinctive in that only the dynamics MLP and reward head are updated; the representation (V-JEPA encoder) remains fixed, making online adaptation extremely lightweight.

\subsection{V-JEPA~2 as Frozen Encoder}

We use V-JEPA~2 ViT-L~\citep{bardes2024vjepa} (325M parameters) as a frozen feature extractor. Each camera frame (224$\times$224 RGB) yields a 384-dimensional embedding $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ after spatial pooling. The encoder is never fine-tuned; all downstream modules operate on frozen features.

Linear probes confirm that V-JEPA embeddings encode task-relevant state information: $R^2 = 0.86$ for object position and $R^2 = 0.89$ for object size/depth (Figure~\ref{fig:probe}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/probe_summary.png}
\caption{\textbf{V-JEPA~2 linear probe results.} Frozen features predict object position ($R^2$=0.86) and size ($R^2$=0.89) well above random baselines, confirming the encoder captures task-relevant state.}
\label{fig:probe}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Dynamics Model}

We train a single dynamics MLP $f_\theta$ to predict the next latent state:
\begin{equation}
    f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) = \mathbf{z}_t + \text{MLP}_\theta([\mathbf{z}_t; \mathbf{a}_t])
\end{equation}
The MLP has architecture $384{+}d_a \rightarrow 512 \rightarrow 512 \rightarrow 384$ with LayerNorm and GELU activations, and a residual connection ($\sim$500K parameters). Training uses MSE loss on frozen V-JEPA embeddings of observed transitions.

\subsection{Task-Aligned Reward Head}

A key finding of this work is that proxy reward signals (e.g., latent-space displacement magnitude) are insufficient for complex tasks. We train a lightweight reward head $g_\phi$ to predict ground-truth environment reward from latent state:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{r}_t = g_\phi(\mathbf{z}_t)
\end{equation}
Architecture: $384 \rightarrow 256 \rightarrow 128 \rightarrow 1$ with LayerNorm and GELU ($\sim$130K parameters). The reward head is trained with MSE loss on transitions labelled with ground-truth reward.

\subsection{CEM Planning}

At each timestep, we use the Cross-Entropy Method~\citep{rubinstein1999cross} to select actions. Given the current latent $\mathbf{z}_t$, CEM samples $K$ candidate action sequences of horizon $H$, rolls them out through the dynamics model, scores each sequence by cumulative predicted reward, and refines the sampling distribution toward elite (top-$k$) sequences over multiple iterations.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{CEM Planning Step}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Current latent $\mathbf{z}_t$, dynamics model $f_\theta$, reward head $g_\phi$
\STATE Initialize $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times d_a}$, $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = 0.5 \cdot \mathbf{1}$
\FOR{$i = 1$ to $N_{\text{iter}}$}
    \STATE Sample $K$ action sequences: $\mathbf{A}^{(m)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2)$, clipped to $[-1, 1]^{d_a}$
    \FOR{each candidate $m$ and horizon step $h$}
        \STATE $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{t+h}^{(m)} = f_\theta(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{t+h-1}^{(m)}, \mathbf{a}_h^{(m)})$
        \STATE $S^{(m)} \mathrel{+}= g_\phi(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{t+h}^{(m)})$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Select top-$k$ elite sequences by score $S^{(m)}$
    \STATE $\boldsymbol{\mu} \leftarrow \text{mean}(\text{elites})$, $\boldsymbol{\sigma} \leftarrow \text{std}(\text{elites})$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Return:} $\boldsymbol{\mu}[0]$ (first action of optimized sequence)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Default hyperparameters: $K=64$ candidates, $k=8$ elites, $N_{\text{iter}}=5$ iterations, horizon $H=8$.

\subsection{Dyna Online Learning Loop}

Following~\citet{sutton1991dyna}, we iterate between acting in the environment and improving the world model:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Evaluate:} Run 10 episodes with CEM planning and 10 with random actions; compute win rate (fraction of episodes where MPC reward $>$ random reward).
    \item \textbf{Collect:} Execute 60 on-policy rollouts (100 steps each) using the current CEM planner, collecting 6000 new labelled transitions.
    \item \textbf{Embed:} Encode all new frames with frozen V-JEPA~2, parallelised across 10 GPU workers ($<$4 min for 6000 frames).
    \item \textbf{Fine-tune:} Update the dynamics model (15 epochs, lr=$5{\times}10^{-5}$) and reward head (20 epochs, lr=$10^{-3}$) on the growing dataset.
    \item \textbf{Repeat} from step 1 for $N$ rounds.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Mixed Replay Buffer.} A critical innovation is the replay buffer composition. Na\"ively training on only on-policy data leads to catastrophic forgetting (Section~\ref{sec:forgetting}). We maintain a mixed buffer of $\sim$20K offline transitions (collected once with a random policy) combined with all on-policy data from each Dyna round. This ensures the dynamics model is always exposed to diverse state-action distributions.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Environment}

All experiments use the \textbf{walker-walk} task from the DeepMind Control Suite~\citep{tassa2018deepmind}. The walker has a 6-dimensional continuous action space controlling 6 actuators. The reward is a product of forward velocity and an upright bonus, making it a challenging locomotion task with sparse high-reward regions.

Camera frames are rendered at $224 \times 224$ RGB and processed by V-JEPA~2 to produce 384-dimensional latent vectors.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

For each Dyna round, we evaluate with 10 paired episodes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MPC agent:} Uses CEM planning with the current dynamics model and reward head.
    \item \textbf{Random baseline:} Executes uniformly random actions.
\end{itemize}
Each pair uses the same random seed (identical initial state). The \textbf{win rate} is the fraction of episodes where the MPC agent accumulates higher reward than the random baseline.

\subsection{Compute}

All experiments run on NVIDIA A10G GPUs via Modal cloud compute. The entire 10-experiment campaign costs under \$30, demonstrating that frozen-encoder MBRL is accessible to individual researchers.

\subsection{Phase 1: Proxy Reward Plateau (Experiments 1--7)}

Experiments 1--7 establish the baseline system and Dyna loop. Using a \emph{proxy} reward signal (magnitude of latent-space displacement, which loosely correlates with locomotion), CEM planning achieves at most 50\% win rate across all configurations---including ensemble dynamics (Exp~6) and multi-round Dyna fine-tuning (Exp~7).

The 50\% ceiling is not a dynamics problem: validation loss decreases monotonically across Dyna rounds ($0.038 \rightarrow 0.034$), confirming the world model is improving. Rather, the proxy reward fails to discriminate between ``walking forward'' and ``falling spectacularly,'' both of which produce large latent displacements.

\label{sec:reward}

To break the proxy-reward ceiling, we train a lightweight MLP reward head ($384 \rightarrow 256 \rightarrow 128 \rightarrow 1$) on transitions labelled with ground-truth environment reward. CEM now plans by maximising predicted reward instead of proxy displacement.

The reward head immediately boosts R0 baseline from 50\% to 70\%. After one Dyna round, R1 achieves \textbf{80\% win rate with MPC reward 8.93}---a $+$30\% improvement over the proxy-reward ceiling (Table~\ref{tab:exp7}).

However, R2 collapses catastrophically: win rate drops from 80\% to 40\%. The dynamics model continues improving (val $0.031 \rightarrow 0.022$), but the reward head \emph{overfits}: val loss increases from 0.106 to 0.142. After re-training on narrow on-policy data, the reward head memorises reward patterns from visited states rather than generalising to the broader action-space explored by CEM.

\subsection{Phase 3: Stabilisation Attempts (Experiment 9)}
\label{sec:forgetting}

To test whether the collapse is caused by the reward head or the dynamics model, Experiment 9 \textbf{freezes the reward head} after R1 and continues only dynamics fine-tuning. Results:

The dynamics val loss decreases (indicating good fit to on-policy data), but the model loses accuracy in regions CEM needs to explore for planning.

This dual diagnosis motivates the replay buffer approach of Experiment~10.

\subsection{Phase 4: Mixed Replay Buffer (Experiment 10)}

Experiment 10 addresses both bottlenecks simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamics regularisation:} A mixed replay buffer combining $\sim$20K offline transitions with growing on-policy data prevents the dynamics model from forgetting the global state space.
    \item \textbf{Reward head re-training:} The reward head is unfrozen and retrained each round on the full labelled dataset, benefiting from greater data diversity.
    \item \textbf{Parallel V-JEPA embedding:} 10 Modal GPU workers embed frames in parallel, reducing embedding time from 40 min to 3.3 min; cached embeddings load in 0.0 min on subsequent runs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reward Head: Optimal Calibration Window}

The reward head exhibits a clear ``Goldilocks zone'' for calibration:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Underfitting} (val $> 0.15$): Reward predictions are too noisy for CEM to find good action sequences (R0 performance).
    \item \textbf{Optimal} (val $\approx 0.07$--$0.09$): Reward predictions guide planning effectively without overfitting to specific trajectories (\textbf{R2 = 100\%}).
    \item \textbf{Overfitting} (val $> 0.12$ after initial decline): The reward head memorises training data and misgrades novel states explored by CEM (R3 regression; Exp~8 R2 collapse).
\end{itemize}

This pattern is consistent across Experiments 8--10 and suggests that reward head early stopping or adaptive learning rate scheduling could sustain peak performance beyond a single optimal round.

\subsection{Scaling: Parallel V-JEPA Embedding}

V-JEPA~2 ViT-L inference is the computational bottleneck: encoding 20K frames takes $\sim$40 minutes on a single A10G GPU. We parallelise across 10 Modal GPU workers, each embedding 2K frames, reducing wall-clock time to \textbf{3.3 minutes} (12$\times$ speedup). The embeddings are cached to a persistent volume; subsequent runs load them in 0.0 minutes. This makes the Dyna loop dominated by dynamics/reward training time ($<$5 min per round) rather than perception.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why frozen V-JEPA works for control.}
V-JEPA~2 is pretrained to predict masked video patches in latent space. This objective implicitly learns to encode: (1)~object positions and configurations (needed for next-frame prediction), (2)~motion patterns and dynamics (needed for temporal prediction), and (3)~visual saliency hierarchies (attention-based encoding prioritises large visual changes). Walker-walk produces large, visually salient limb movements that fall squarely within V-JEPA's learned feature space.

\paragraph{Why 100\% is achievable with only 10 episodes.}
The 100\% win rate does not mean the MPC agent dominates every episode---rather, it outperforms a \emph{random} baseline in all 10 evaluation episodes. The MPC rewards at R2 (avg 6.65) remain modest compared to expert-level performance on walker-walk ($>$900 cumulative reward per episode). The result demonstrates \emph{consistent planning superiority}, not mastery.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reward head instability:} Performance peaks for one round then declines. Early stopping or regularisation of the reward head is needed for sustained performance.
    \item \textbf{Single task:} We demonstrate the full pipeline only on walker-walk. Earlier experiments (1--5) show the approach works on reacher-easy but fails on visually subtle tasks (finger-spin, point-mass).
    \item \textbf{Random baseline:} Win rate against a random agent is a weak metric. Comparison to trained RL baselines (Dreamer, SAC) would better characterise absolute performance.
    \item \textbf{Absolute reward:} MPC rewards of 5--9 per episode are far below the $>$900 achievable by trained policies. The approach demonstrates proof-of-concept, not competitive performance.
    \item \textbf{Frozen encoder:} Tasks requiring fine-grained visual discrimination (e.g., finger rotation, small point mass on uniform background) fail because V-JEPA features do not capture the relevant state.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future work.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Freeze reward head at peak:} Combine Exp~9's frozen-reward concept with Exp~10's replay buffer to sustain 100\% beyond R2.
    \item \textbf{Reward head regularisation:} Weight decay, learning rate warmup, or EMA updates to prevent overfitting.
    \item \textbf{Stronger baselines:} Compare against Dreamer, TD-MPC, and SAC on the same walker-walk evaluation protocol.
    \item \textbf{Real robot transfer:} Deploy on physical hardware where V-JEPA's internet-video pretraining may provide stronger visual priors.
    \item \textbf{Multi-task scaling:} Extend the replay buffer across multiple DMC tasks for a single dynamics model with task-conditioned reward heads.
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We have demonstrated a complete pipeline for model-based reinforcement learning using frozen video foundation model features. Starting from V-JEPA~2---a model pretrained on internet video with no robot data---we learn lightweight dynamics ($\sim$500K params) and reward ($\sim$130K params) modules in the frozen latent space. Through 10 progressive experiments, we identify and resolve three key bottlenecks: reward misalignment (solved by a learned reward head, $+$30\% win rate), reward overfitting (characterised through ablation), and dynamics forgetting (solved by a mixed replay buffer).

Our final system achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} on walker-walk against a random baseline. While this is far from expert-level performance, it represents the first demonstration that video foundation models pretrained for visual understanding contain representations sufficient for continuous control---without any task-specific encoder training, and at a total compute cost under \$30.

The key insight is that \emph{representation quality determines planning quality}. When a frozen encoder captures task-relevant visual features, simple dynamics models and CEM planning suffice for control. The remaining challenge is not representation learning but \emph{online model stability}: preventing small learned modules from overfitting during the Dyna learning loop.
