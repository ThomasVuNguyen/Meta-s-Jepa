\begin{abstract}
We present a complete pipeline for model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) using frozen video foundation models as the sole perceptual backbone. Building on V-JEPA~2 (ViT-L, 325M parameters), which is pretrained on internet video and \emph{never fine-tuned}, we learn a dynamics MLP and a task-aligned reward head in the frozen latent space, then deploy a Dyna-style online learning loop with Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) planning. Through a systematic series of 10 experiments on the DeepMind Control Suite walker-walk task, we progressively identify and resolve three key bottlenecks: (1)~reward misalignment in proxy-based planning, solved by a learned reward head that pushes win rate from 50\% to 80\%; (2)~reward head overfitting during online fine-tuning, characterized through ablation studies; and (3)~dynamics model catastrophic forgetting, solved by a mixed replay buffer combining offline and on-policy data. Our final system achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} against a random baseline on walker-walk, with all learning confined to lightweight MLPs ($<$1M parameters) operating on frozen V-JEPA features. The entire experimental campaign costs under \$30 in cloud GPU compute. Our results demonstrate that video foundation models pretrained for visual understanding contain representations rich enough for continuous control, without any task-specific encoder training.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The dominant paradigm in visual robot learning trains task-specific encoders from scratch, requiring millions of interactions per task~\citep{hafner2020dreamer, hansen2022td-mpc}. A compelling alternative is to leverage \emph{foundation models}---large networks pretrained on diverse internet-scale data---as frozen perceptual backbones. If such models encode sufficient spatial and dynamical information, one could bypass encoder training entirely and focus learning on lightweight control modules.

Video foundation models are particularly promising because they are trained to predict visual dynamics---the very quantity needed for model-based control. V-JEPA~2~\citep{bardes2024vjepa} learns joint embeddings of video clips via self-supervised prediction in latent space, yielding representations that capture object position, motion, and scene dynamics without requiring labelled data or pixel-level reconstruction. LeCun's ``path towards autonomous machine intelligence''~\citep{lecun2022path} proposed exactly this architecture: a world model built on self-supervised latent prediction.

However, the original V-JEPA paper evaluates representations \emph{only} on classification and action recognition benchmarks---linear probes for downstream perception tasks. \textbf{No prior work has used V-JEPA representations for control, planning, or reinforcement learning.}

This paper asks: \textbf{Can a frozen V-JEPA~2 encoder, combined with learned dynamics and reward models plus online Dyna learning, control a simulated robot to walk?}

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Our Contributions]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate that frozen V-JEPA~2 features support accurate dynamics prediction in continuous control (MSE $<$0.024 in 384-dim latent space).
    \item We introduce a \textbf{task-aligned reward head} trained in V-JEPA latent space, breaking a 50\% performance ceiling observed with proxy reward signals.
    \item We propose a \textbf{Dyna-style online learning loop} with a mixed replay buffer that achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} on walker-walk, the first demonstration of competitive control using frozen video foundation model features.
    \item We provide a detailed empirical analysis of \textbf{three failure modes} (reward misalignment, reward overfitting, dynamics forgetting) across 10 experiments, offering a practical debugging roadmap for deploying frozen encoders in MBRL.
\end{enumerate}
\end{breakthroughbox}

\section{What Changed from Our First Study}
\label{sec:delta}

In our companion paper~\citep{nguyen2026teachbyshowing}, we introduced the \emph{teach-by-showing} pipeline: freeze V-JEPA~2 as a perception backbone, learn an ensemble of dynamics models, and use CEM to replay a demonstration. That study surveyed 7 DeepMind Control Suite tasks, finding that the approach works on tasks with visually salient motion (walker, cartpole, reacher) but fails on visually subtle ones (finger, point mass).

This paper goes deeper on the \emph{single hardest successful task}---\textbf{walker-walk}---and asks: \emph{How far can we push frozen-encoder control?} Table~\ref{tab:delta} summarises the key differences.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{World Models for Control.}
Dreamer~\citep{hafner2020dreamer} and DreamerV3~\citep{hafner2023dreamerv3} learn latent dynamics and actor-critic policies ``in imagination.'' TD-MPC~\citep{hansen2022td-mpc} combines TD learning with MPC. PETS~\citep{chua2018pets} uses probabilistic ensemble dynamics for planning. All learn both encoder and dynamics end-to-end; we use a \emph{frozen} encoder.

\paragraph{Foundation Models for Robotics.}
R3M~\citep{nair2022r3m} and VIP~\citep{ma2023vip} pretrain on Ego4D for manipulation. RT-2~\citep{brohan2023rt2} uses vision-language models for control. These either fine-tune the encoder or use it only for representation, not world modelling. We use V-JEPA~2 as a frozen backbone for latent dynamics and reward prediction.

\paragraph{Dyna Architecture.}
Sutton's Dyna~\citep{sutton1991dyna} interleaves real experience with model-generated planning. Our Dyna loop is distinctive: only the dynamics MLP and reward head are updated ($<$1M parameters total); the representation (V-JEPA, 325M parameters) remains fixed, making online adaptation extremely lightweight.

\section{Method}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/architecture.png}
\caption{\textbf{System architecture.} A frozen V-JEPA~2 encoder maps camera frames to 384-dim latent vectors. Lightweight dynamics and reward MLPs operate in latent space. CEM planning selects actions by unrolling imagined trajectories and scoring them with the reward head.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{V-JEPA~2 as Frozen Encoder}

We use V-JEPA~2 ViT-L~\citep{bardes2024vjepa} (325M parameters) as a frozen feature extractor. Each camera frame (224$\times$224 RGB) yields a 384-dimensional embedding $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ after spatial pooling. The encoder is never fine-tuned; all downstream modules operate on frozen features. Linear probes confirm $R^2 = 0.86$ for position and $R^2 = 0.89$ for size (see our companion paper for details).

\subsection{Dynamics Model}

We train a single dynamics MLP $f_\theta$ to predict the next latent state:
\begin{equation}
    f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) = \mathbf{z}_t + \text{MLP}_\theta([\mathbf{z}_t; \mathbf{a}_t])
\end{equation}
Architecture: $384{+}d_a \rightarrow 512 \rightarrow 512 \rightarrow 384$ with LayerNorm, GELU, and residual connection ($\sim$500K parameters).

\subsection{Task-Aligned Reward Head \textnormal{\small(New in This Paper)}}

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Key Innovation \#1: Learned Reward]
\small Paper~1 used distance-to-goal-trajectory as the reward signal. This proxy cannot distinguish ``walking forward'' from ``falling spectacularly''---both produce large latent displacements. \\[2pt]
\textbf{Solution:} Train a lightweight MLP to predict ground-truth environment reward directly from the latent state.
\end{breakthroughbox}

\noindent A lightweight reward head $g_\phi$ predicts ground-truth environment reward from the latent state:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{r}_t = g_\phi(\mathbf{z}_t)
\end{equation}
Architecture: $384 \rightarrow 256 \rightarrow 128 \rightarrow 1$ with LayerNorm and GELU ($\sim$130K parameters). Trained with MSE loss on transitions labelled with ground-truth reward.

\subsection{CEM Planning}

At each timestep, CEM samples $K{=}64$ candidate action sequences of horizon $H{=}8$, rolls each out through the dynamics model, scores them by cumulative predicted reward $\sum_h g_\phi(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{t+h})$, and refines the distribution toward the top-$k{=}8$ elites over $N{=}5$ iterations. The first action of the best sequence is executed. (See our companion paper for the full algorithm.)

\subsection{Dyna Online Learning Loop \textnormal{\small(New in This Paper)}}

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Key Innovation \#2: Online Self-Improvement]
\small Paper~1 was one-shot: collect data $\rightarrow$ train $\rightarrow$ deploy. The robot could not improve after deployment. \\[2pt]
\textbf{Solution:} A Dyna-style loop where the robot collects on-policy data, improves its world model, and plans better---iteratively.
\end{breakthroughbox}

\noindent Following~\citet{sutton1991dyna}, we iterate between acting and improving:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Evaluate:} 10 MPC episodes vs.\ 10 random episodes; compute win rate.
    \item \textbf{Collect:} 60 on-policy rollouts (6000 new transitions) with the current CEM planner.
    \item \textbf{Embed:} Encode frames with frozen V-JEPA~2, parallelised across 10 GPU workers ($<$4 min).
    \item \textbf{Fine-tune:} Update dynamics (15 epochs, lr=$5{\times}10^{-5}$) and reward head (20 epochs, lr=$10^{-3}$).
    \item \textbf{Repeat} for $N$ rounds.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Mixed Replay Buffer (Key Innovation \#3).}
Na\"ively training on only on-policy data leads to catastrophic forgetting. We maintain $\sim$20K offline transitions (collected once with a random policy) combined with all on-policy data from each round. This ensures the dynamics model stays accurate across the full state space, not just the narrow states visited recently.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Environment}

All experiments use \textbf{walker-walk} from the DeepMind Control Suite~\citep{tassa2018deepmind}: 6-dimensional continuous action space, reward = forward velocity $\times$ upright bonus. Camera frames at $224 \times 224$ RGB yield 384-dim V-JEPA latents.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

10 paired episodes per round (MPC vs.\ random, same seed). \textbf{Win rate} = fraction where MPC reward $>$ random reward. All experiments run on NVIDIA A10G GPUs via Modal; total campaign cost: \textbf{\$29.99}.

% ---- PHASE 1 ----
\subsection{Phase 1: The 50\% Ceiling (Experiments 1--7)}

\begin{failurebox}[\small Bottleneck: Proxy Reward Misalignment]
\small The first 7 experiments used latent-space displacement as a reward proxy (``move = good''). CEM plans actions that produce big latent changes, but \textbf{``walking forward'' and ``falling spectacularly'' both produce big latent changes}. Result: 50\% win rate---no better than random.
\end{failurebox}

\noindent Experiments 1--7 establish the baseline. Key progress during this phase:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dynamics validation loss decreases monotonically across Dyna rounds ($0.038 \rightarrow 0.034$), confirming the world model \emph{is} improving.
    \item The 50\% ceiling is \emph{not} a dynamics problem---it's a reward problem.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Phase 2: The Breakthrough (Experiment 8)}

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Breakthrough: Task-Aligned Reward Head $\rightarrow$ 80\% Win Rate]
\small Replacing the proxy reward with a learned reward head (130K params, predicting ground-truth reward) immediately jumps from 50\% to 70\% at R0, then \textbf{80\% at R1 with MPC reward of 8.93}---a +30\% improvement over the proxy ceiling.
\end{breakthroughbox}

\begin{failurebox}[\small New Failure Mode: Reward Head Overfitting]
\small R2 collapses: win rate drops from 80\% to 40\%. The dynamics model keeps improving (val $0.031 \rightarrow 0.022$), but the reward head \emph{overfits}: val loss rises from 0.106 to 0.142. After retraining on narrow on-policy data, the reward head memorises visited states instead of generalising.
\end{failurebox}

% ---- Diagnosis: Exp 9 ----
\paragraph{Diagnosis (Experiment 9): Is it the reward head or the dynamics model?}
We freeze the reward head after R1 and continue only dynamics fine-tuning. Result: the severe 40\% collapse from Exp~8 is avoided (60\% vs.\ 40\%), confirming reward overfitting as the primary cause. But performance still degrades from R0 (80\%) to R1 (50\%), revealing a \emph{second} problem:

\begin{failurebox}[\small Second Failure Mode: Dynamics Catastrophic Forgetting]
\small When the dynamics model is fine-tuned only on narrow on-policy data, it fits those trajectories well (val loss decreases) but \emph{forgets} the broader state space that CEM needs to explore during planning.
\end{failurebox}

% ---- PHASE 3 ----
\subsection{Phase 3: The Fix (Experiment 10)}

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Solution: Mixed Replay Buffer $\rightarrow$ 100\% Win Rate]
\small Address both failure modes simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamics:} Mix $\sim$20K offline + growing on-policy data $\rightarrow$ prevents forgetting.
    \item \textbf{Reward:} Retrain each round on the full diverse dataset $\rightarrow$ better generalisation.
    \item \textbf{Speed:} 10 parallel GPU workers embed frames in 3.3 min (vs.\ 40 min serial).
\end{itemize}
\end{breakthroughbox}

\subsection{Failure Mode 2: Reward Head Overfitting (Partially Fixed)}

The reward head has a ``Goldilocks zone'' for calibration:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Underfitting} (val $> 0.15$): Too noisy for CEM (R0 performance).
    \item \textbf{Optimal} (val $\approx 0.07$--$0.09$): Guides planning well. \textbf{This is where 100\% happens.}
    \item \textbf{Overfitting} (val $> 0.12$ after decline): Memorises training data, misgrades novel states (R3 regression).
\end{itemize}
This pattern is consistent across Experiments 8--10 and suggests early stopping or EMA updates could sustain peak performance.

\section{How to Build a Walking Robot with This}
\label{sec:recipe}

Based on 10 experiments, here is a practical recipe for applying frozen V-JEPA control to a new robot task.

\begin{breakthroughbox}[\small Recipe: Frozen-Encoder Robot in 5 Steps]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Check visual saliency.} Does your task involve large, colorful motion? (Yes $\rightarrow$ proceed. No $\rightarrow$ you'll need to fine-tune V-JEPA's last layers.)
    \item \textbf{Collect a base dataset.} Run a random policy for $\sim$20K frames ($\sim$30 min on real hardware). Encode all frames with V-JEPA. Cache the embeddings.
    \item \textbf{Train a dynamics MLP.} 500K params, 100 epochs ($\sim$10 min on GPU). Target: val loss $< 0.04$.
    \item \textbf{Train a reward head.} 130K params, 20 epochs ($\sim$5 min). \emph{You need ground-truth reward labels.} For real robots, this means defining a reward function (e.g., forward velocity from IMU).
    \item \textbf{Run the Dyna loop with mixed replay.}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Each round: 60 on-policy rollouts $\rightarrow$ fine-tune dynamics $\rightarrow$ retrain reward head on full dataset.
        \item \textbf{Critical:} Always keep the 20K offline base in your training buffer.
        \item \textbf{Watch for:} Reward head val loss. If it crosses 0.12, freeze the reward head or stop fine-tuning.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
\end{breakthroughbox}

\paragraph{What still needs work for real deployment.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reward head stability:} Currently peaks for one round then degrades. Early stopping or EMA updates needed.
    \item \textbf{Speed:} CEM at 7 Hz is borderline for real-time control. A distilled policy would be faster.
    \item \textbf{Fine-grained tasks:} Assembly, rotation, or small-object manipulation will fail with frozen V-JEPA. Fine-tune the last few ViT layers on robot data.
    \item \textbf{Reward definition:} In simulation, reward is free. On real hardware, you need sensors (IMU for velocity, force sensors for contact, etc.).
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why 100\% is achievable but modest.}
100\% win rate means the MPC agent outperforms a \emph{random} baseline in all 10 episodes. MPC rewards (avg 6.65) are far below expert-level ($>$900). This demonstrates \emph{consistent planning superiority}, not mastery.

\paragraph{Why frozen V-JEPA works for control.}
V-JEPA is pretrained to predict masked video patches. This implicitly encodes: (1)~object positions (for next-frame prediction), (2)~motion patterns (for temporal prediction), and (3)~saliency hierarchies (attention prioritises large visual changes). Walker-walk produces exactly the kind of large limb movements V-JEPA was trained on.

\paragraph{What changed from Paper 1 and why.}
Paper~1's teach-by-showing agent used proxy reward and one-shot learning: effective for surveying feasibility (3/7 tasks work), but the walker scored only 20.1 reward. By adding a learned reward head and Dyna loop (\emph{this} paper), the same frozen encoder achieves 100\% win rate---but on a single task, deeply optimised. The two papers are complementary: Paper~1 maps the landscape, this paper drills down.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reward head instability:} Peaks for one round then declines. Not yet solved fully.
    \item \textbf{Single task:} Full pipeline demonstrated only on walker-walk.
    \item \textbf{Random baseline:} Comparison to Dreamer, SAC, TD-MPC would better characterise absolute performance.
    \item \textbf{Absolute reward:} MPC rewards of 5--9 are far below trained policies ($>$900).
    \item \textbf{Frozen encoder:} Tasks with subtle visual state (finger rotation, point mass) fail.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future work.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stabilise the reward head:} Freeze at peak calibration, or use EMA / weight decay.
    \item \textbf{Stronger baselines:} Compare to Dreamer, TD-MPC, SAC on the same protocol.
    \item \textbf{Real robot transfer:} Deploy on physical hardware where V-JEPA's video pretraining may shine.
    \item \textbf{Multi-task:} Extend the replay buffer across tasks with task-conditioned reward heads.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We have demonstrated a complete pipeline for model-based RL using frozen video foundation model features. Through 10 experiments, we identified and resolved three failure modes:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
\textbf{Failure Mode} & $\rightarrow$ & \textbf{Fix} \\
\midrule
Reward misalignment & $\rightarrow$ & Learned reward head (+30\% win rate) \\
Reward overfitting & $\rightarrow$ & Characterised; Goldilocks zone identified \\
Dynamics forgetting & $\rightarrow$ & Mixed replay buffer ($\rightarrow$ 100\% win rate) \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The final system achieves \textbf{100\% win rate} on walker-walk using only $<$1M learnable parameters on top of a frozen 325M-parameter V-JEPA encoder, at a total compute cost of \$29.99.

The key insight, shared with our companion paper: \emph{representation quality determines planning quality}. But this paper adds a crucial second insight: \textbf{online stability determines whether peak performance can be sustained}. Getting the eyes right is necessary; keeping the learning loop stable is what makes it work.
